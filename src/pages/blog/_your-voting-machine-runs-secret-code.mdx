# Your Voting Machine Runs Secret Code

Here is a fact that should bother everyone, regardless of party: **two private companies count approximately 80% of American votes** using software that no member of the public, no independent researcher, and no elected official has ever been allowed to fully examine.

Election Systems & Software. Dominion Voting Systems. Between them, they supply the machines, the software, and the back-end tabulation systems for elections in nearly every state in the country. Their source code is proprietary. Their algorithms are trade secrets. Their systems are certified by federal testing laboratories — but those labs sign non-disclosure agreements before they're allowed to look.

You vote on it. You are not allowed to see it.

This is not a conspiracy theory. It is a procurement policy. And it is exactly the kind of problem that public code exists to solve.

## How We Got Here

The Help America Vote Act of 2002 was passed in the wake of the Florida recount disaster. Congress wanted to modernize elections and create consistent federal standards. It established the Election Assistance Commission and set up a voluntary certification process for voting systems.

What it did not do was require that certified systems be open source. That omission — a quiet one, barely debated at the time — handed the American election infrastructure to a handful of private vendors and gave them something extraordinary: **a legal monopoly on the most important software in a democracy, with no obligation to let the public see how it works.**

The certification process requires vendors to submit their systems for testing. The testing labs — accredited by the EAC — examine them. But the results of those tests are largely confidential. The source code is not published. When states purchase these systems, they sign license agreements that prohibit them from disclosing the software to third parties, including independent security researchers.

So the county elections official who bought the machines doesn't own them. Doesn't own the code. Can't have an independent expert look at them. And if anything goes wrong — a miscounted precinct, an unexplained variance, a system crash on election night — the vendor controls the forensic investigation of its own product.

## What Security Researchers Have Found Anyway

Despite these restrictions, security researchers have managed to examine some voting systems — through leaked code, decommissioned machines purchased secondhand, and, occasionally, court-ordered disclosure.

What they found was not reassuring.

- In 2019, a team of researchers at the **DEF CON Voting Village** — an annual conference where hackers examine election equipment — found that systems from ES&S, Dominion, and other vendors contained exploitable vulnerabilities, outdated operating systems, and in some cases remote-access software that vendors had previously denied existed.
- In 2020, a security firm hired by a county in Georgia found that Dominion systems were running **Windows 7** — an operating system Microsoft had ended support for in January of that year.
- A 2022 report by a Michigan forensics firm found numerous security issues in Dominion ImageCast Evolution machines, including vulnerabilities that could allow an attacker with physical access to manipulate results.
- Researchers at the University of Michigan found that **ES&S ExpressPoll** pollbook software exposed sensitive voter data because it stored information insecurely.

None of these findings required sophisticated nation-state hacking. They required people being allowed — often barely — to look at systems that were already deployed, already counting votes, already certified.

**Imagine what a full, open audit would find. Then ask why we aren't doing one.**

## "Trade Secret" Is Not a Defense for Democracy

The vendors have a ready answer: their software is a trade secret, and revealing it would help bad actors attack it.

This argument fails on its face.

Security through obscurity is not security. Every serious cybersecurity professional knows this. The strongest systems in the world — the cryptography that secures your bank account, the protocols that run the internet — are open. They are open because the security community learned, decades ago, that hidden systems accumulate vulnerabilities that nobody finds until it's too late. **Transparency is what creates accountability, and accountability is what creates trustworthy systems.**

Keeping voting software secret doesn't protect elections. It protects vendors from scrutiny. Those are different things.

The federal government's own Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) has said that **hand-marked paper ballots** are the most secure method of voting — precisely because they create a physical record that exists independently of any software. When CISA recommends a paper record as a check on digital systems, it is implicitly acknowledging that the digital systems cannot be fully trusted without one.

That is the official position of the federal election security agency: our voting machines need a non-digital backup, because we can't fully verify the machines.

## The States That Are Doing This Right

A few states have moved toward the open, auditable approach that election security experts actually recommend.

**Colorado** has become the national model. The state requires hand-marked paper ballots, post-election risk-limiting audits that statistically verify results, and has invested in election administration transparency that goes well beyond federal minimums. Colorado's elections are among the most trusted in the country — not because Coloradans trust the machines, but because the process doesn't require trusting the machines.

**The Open Source Election Technology (OSET) Institute** has spent years building **ElectOS** — an open-source election technology platform designed to replace proprietary voting systems with software that any state, any researcher, any citizen can examine. It's funded partly by federal grants. It is not yet widely deployed, because the procurement relationships between states and established vendors are deeply entrenched.

That entrenchment is the problem. ES&S and Dominion have spent years building relationships with state and local election officials, navigating certification processes that take years and cost millions, and embedding themselves into election infrastructure in ways that make switching painful. Open source alternatives have to fight uphill against that incumbency — not because they're less secure, but because the market was structured before they existed.

## What Public Code Looks Like for Elections

The fix is not complicated to describe. It is complicated to achieve politically — which is exactly why this movement exists.

**Voting software funded by public money should be public code.** Every line of it. Every update to it. Certified not just by a federal lab under NDA, but by the open security research community that has proven, repeatedly, that it finds vulnerabilities faster and more reliably than closed review.

This is not a radical position. It is the standard that every serious election security researcher endorses. It is the standard that the countries with the most trusted elections — Canada, Germany, the Netherlands — either meet or are moving toward. It is what democracy requires.

When you vote, you are exercising the most fundamental act of democratic participation. The software that records and counts that vote should be the most transparent code in the country.

Instead, it's the most secret.

That is a policy choice. We can make a different one.

## The Broader Point

Elections are the sharpest example of a problem that runs through every layer of American government. From [the algorithms that deny your benefits](/blog/the-code-that-decides-your-life) to [the IT contracts that eat your tax dollars and answer to no one](/blog/the-black-box-contracting-problem), the pattern is the same: **public money. Public decisions. Private code.**

The voting machine is just the version of that problem that most clearly shows what's at stake. Democracy is not an abstraction. It runs on software. And right now, that software belongs to two companies in Omaha and Denver.

It should belong to you.

## What You Can Do

1. **[Sign the petition.](/sign)** Demand that publicly funded software — including election software — be publicly owned.

2. **[Contact your representatives.](/campaign)** Ask where they stand on open-source election technology. Ask who funds their campaigns. Ask if those are related questions.

3. **Know your state's ballot type.** If your state uses hand-marked paper ballots and conducts risk-limiting audits, support the officials who maintained those standards. If it doesn't, that's a conversation worth having.

4. **Share this post.** Most people assume someone is auditing this code. Nobody elected is.

The ballots are yours. The software should be too.
